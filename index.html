Formula Reference
① Inflation-Adjusted Tuition (per year)
Cost_year_k = C₀ × (1 + i)^(n + k) C₀ = tuition today i = education inflation rate n = years until college starts k = 0, 1, 2, … (year of college: 1st, 2nd, …)
Each year of college costs more than the previous because inflation continues during the college years, not just before them. Year 1 is already inflated by n years. Year 2 by n+1. Year 3 by n+2. And so on.
② Total Education Fund
Total = Σ C₀ × (1 + i)^(n + k) for k = 0 to (d − 1) d = college duration in years
The total is the sum of all inflation-adjusted annual tuitions over the full college duration. This is the lump sum you need at the start of college to fully cover all fees — assuming funds not yet spent continue to sit idle (conservative, no growth assumed during college).
③ Why No Growth During College?
Conservative assumption: 0% return during drawdown (All funds parked safely when child enters college)
A more aggressive model would assume the remaining fund earns returns while tuition is being paid year by year — which would reduce the lump sum needed. This calculator uses the conservative approach (full sum needed upfront) to avoid underestimating. The result is a safe upper-bound target.
Why This Computation Is Accurate
⚡ 4 Reasons This Is Near-Exact
📈
Inflation Compounds During College TooMost calculators inflate tuition only to year 1 and multiply by duration. This calculator correctly compounds inflation through every year of college — Year 2 is more expensive than Year 1, Year 3 more than Year 2.
🎯
Education-Specific Inflation RatesGeneral CPI inflation (~3%) significantly understates education cost growth. Philippines private university fees grow ~8–10% p.a. Singapore varies by student type (citizen vs PR vs international). This calculator uses separate, tuition-specific rates by country.
🛡️
Conservative (Safe Upper Bound)The lump sum target assumes zero investment return during the college drawdown period. This is the worst-case requirement. If your fund earns returns while your child is in college, your actual gap is smaller — never larger.
